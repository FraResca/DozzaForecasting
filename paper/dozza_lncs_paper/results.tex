\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\red{All results in this section refer to the final chronological test split,
unless rolling validation is explicitly mentioned. The temporal baselines are
causal: the last-hour baseline uses the last value available before the
forecast origin, and the train/test split uses an embargo equal to the forecast
horizon. Positive MAE gain means that the feature-based model has lower MAE
than the last-hour baseline; positive ablation $\Delta$MAE means that removing
the feature or feature family increases MAE.}

\red{Under this stricter evaluation, the main result changes substantially:
feature-based models are no longer compared with an overly optimistic
persistence baseline. Pedestrian-flow models improve over last-hour persistence
at all tested horizons, and the improvement is largest at short and medium
horizons. For TIM-derived demographic targets, feature-based models also often
beat last-hour persistence on the final test split, although rolling validation
remains more conservative and still favors persistence at the one-hour horizon.}

\red{For entrances, Extra Trees reduces MAE by 45.1\% relative to last-hour
persistence and lowers WAPE from 69.3\% to 38.1\%. For exits, the MAE reduction
is 49.2\%, with WAPE decreasing from 56.8\% to 28.9\%. The same model also
improves $R^2$ for both targets. This supports a stronger claim than in the
previous evaluation: with causal baselines, contextual and historical features
provide clear short-horizon gains for pedestrian-flow forecasting.}

event, and weather variables. Age models select mainly target-history and TIM
variables, plus one weather feature.}

nationality models at every horizon, in age models from 3 to 12 hours, and in

event ablation effect is positive for some settings, especially flow at three
hours and nationality at one and three hours, but it is negative for other
settings. Events are therefore useful exploratory covariates, but the present
encoding does not support a general causal or uniformly beneficial claim.}

\red{SHAP summaries support the same cautious interpretation. Some event
variables rank highly in individual fitted models: for example, an event
rolling feature is the top SHAP feature for flow entrances at three hours,
sport/motor event intensity is the top feature for Italian TIM presence at
three hours, and event-intensity variables rank first for several age targets
between 6 and 12 hours. These local rankings show that the event representation
is used by some models. They do not, by themselves, prove that events improve
forecast accuracy in a stable way across tracks and horizons.}

\red{The main operational result is therefore clear but bounded. For
pedestrian-flow forecasting, the multi-source feature models outperform a
strict causal last-hour baseline over all evaluated horizons. For demographic
TIM targets, the final test split shows many feature-based improvements over
last-hour persistence, but one-hour rolling validation still favors
persistence. Event variables should be retained as exploratory predictors and
diagnostic information, while stronger event conclusions require richer and
more complete event data.}
